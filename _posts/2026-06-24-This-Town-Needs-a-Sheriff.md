I have been thinking about open standards for a long time.
It is kind of baked into what I do.
OpenFeature, flagd, CNCF — most of my open source work lives in that space where the community agrees on a contract so that the ecosystem can grow around it.

So when the Agentic AI Foundation announced their ambassador program, it felt like the right moment to get involved.

<!--more-->

The agentic AI space right now feels like the wild west.
A new framework every week.
New concepts, new acronyms, new opinions about the right way to build.
Some of it is genuinely good thinking.
A lot of it is noise.
And it is very hard to tell which is which when everything arrives at the same velocity.

I think about the early container ecosystem.
Before CNCF there was a similar energy — exciting, chaotic, and deeply fragmented.
What the foundation did was not slow things down.
It gave the community a place to converge.
Project maturity indicators.
Vendor-neutral governance.
A landscape that helped you navigate.
Not a monopoly on ideas, but a shared ground beneath them.

That is what the agentic space is missing.
AAIF is the institution stepping into that gap — the foundation trying to bring order without killing the energy.
This town needs a sheriff.
And I want to be a deputy.

MCP, Goose, AGENTS.md — these are early signals that the community wants shared contracts, not just shared enthusiasm.
I already work with most of these technologies.
Goose is still on my todo list, admittedly.
But the direction is right.

What I am most curious about is not the tools themselves.
It is what open standards enable downstream.

In the CNCF world, standards did not just make tooling interoperable.
They made teams interoperable.
A platform engineer moving between companies could reason about the stack.
A new hire could orient faster.
Shared vocabulary reduced the tax of starting from scratch every time.

I think the same thing will happen here — eventually.
The agentic space will settle.
Some approaches will consolidate.
Others will fade.
What survives will probably look more like protocols than products.

AAIF being a Linux Foundation project is not a small thing.
It signals seriousness about governance.
About longevity.
About the difference between a project with a roadmap and a framework with a VC behind it.

I do not know exactly what the ambassadorship will produce yet.
One public contribution a month.
Talks, content, community.
That is the commitment, and I am happy to make it.

What I do know is that the conversations worth having right now — about how teams work with agents, about what shared understanding looks like before the agent runs, about what we are actually optimizing for — need a place to happen.
Not just on Twitter.
Not just in individual blog posts.
In a community with enough shared vocabulary to make the arguments productive.

I am looking forward to working with a lot of talented and inspiring people to build exactly that.
